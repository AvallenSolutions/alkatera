**In short:** The Greenhouse Gas Protocol is the rulebook almost everyone uses to count carbon. When someone mentions Scope 1, 2 or 3, they are using GHG Protocol language, whether they know it or not.

## What it is

The GHG Protocol is a partnership between the World Resources Institute and the World Business Council for Sustainable Development. Since 2001 it has published free, publicly available standards that define what counts as an emission, whose emissions they are, and how to report them. It is not a law or a certification; it is the common method that laws, certifications and customer questionnaires build on.

## The main standards

- **Corporate Accounting and Reporting Standard** (the "Corporate Standard"): how to measure a whole organisation's [[carbon-footprint]]. It created the split into [[scope-1-emissions|Scope 1]], [[scope-2-emissions|Scope 2]] and [[scope-3-emissions|Scope 3]].
- **Scope 2 Guidance** (2015): the rules for purchased electricity, including the requirement to report both figures explained in [[market-based-vs-location-based]].
- **Corporate Value Chain (Scope 3) Standard** (2011): how to account for the 15 value-chain categories described in [[scope-3-categories]].
- **Product Life Cycle Accounting and Reporting Standard** (2011): how to measure a single product's footprint, the basis for a [[product-carbon-footprint]].

There are also calculation guidance documents and sector tools that help apply the standards in practice using published emission factors.

## Why it matters

Nearly everything downstream assumes GHG Protocol accounting: [[science-based-targets|science-based targets]], CDP disclosure, UK [[secr|SECR]] reporting, and the European [[csrd|CSRD]] climate disclosures all reference or align with it. If a supermarket, distributor or investor asks for your emissions, this is the method they expect.

## The standards are being updated

The first major revision since the standards were written is under way. All four core documents are being reviewed together. The Scope 2 Guidance went through public consultation ending in January 2026, further consultation drafts for the Corporate and Scope 3 Standards are expected during 2026, and final updated editions are expected around the end of 2027. Expect stricter rules on data quality, completeness and disclosure. Nothing you measure today is wasted, but keep your method documented so you can adjust.

## What this means for drinks businesses

- You do not need to read the standards cover to cover. Understand the three scopes, then follow the method your platform or consultant applies.
- Use the free GHG Protocol guidance to sense-check anyone selling you a "proprietary methodology". Credible carbon accounting is not proprietary.
- Record your organisational boundary, reporting year and data sources. Consistent method matters more than any single number, especially with data quality improving year on year.
- When the revised editions land, expect some restated baselines across the industry. That is normal and better than pretending old numbers were perfect.
