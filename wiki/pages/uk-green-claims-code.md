**In short:** The Green Claims Code is the UK competition regulator's rulebook for environmental claims in marketing. Since April 2025 the Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) can decide for itself that a claim is misleading and fine the business up to 10 per cent of worldwide turnover, without going to court first.

## What it is

Published by the CMA in September 2021, the Code explains how consumer protection law applies to environmental claims. It sets six principles: claims must be truthful and accurate; clear and unambiguous; must not omit or hide important information; comparisons must be fair and meaningful; claims must consider the full life cycle of the product; and claims must be substantiated with evidence. The Code itself is guidance, but the law behind it is binding, and the CMA folded its approach into broader unfair commercial practices guidance published in April 2025.

## Who it applies to

Jurisdiction: the United Kingdom. It applies to any business making environmental claims to UK consumers, of any size and in any sector, whether the claim is on packaging, a website, social media or an advert. The Advertising Standards Authority applies parallel rules to advertising.

## Key dates

- September 2021: Green Claims Code published.
- May 2024: the Digital Markets, Competition and Consumers (DMCC) Act received Royal Assent.
- 6 April 2025: the DMCC Act's direct consumer enforcement regime commenced. The CMA can now itself determine breaches and impose fines of up to 10 per cent of global annual turnover.

## Current status

Fully in force. The CMA has said early enforcement focuses on the most egregious breaches, such as objectively false claims, but vague and unsubstantiated green claims are squarely within its published priorities. This is no longer a regime where the worst outcome is being asked to change your wording.

## What this means for drinks businesses

Words like "eco-friendly", "sustainable", "green" or "planet-positive" on a label or website need evidence behind them, held before you publish, covering the whole life cycle of the product. A "[[carbon-neutral]]" claim resting on [[carbon-offsetting|offsets]] is high risk; a recyclability claim must reflect what actually happens to the packaging, not what is theoretically possible (see [[recycled-content]]). The safe pattern is specific, verifiable statements backed by data: a [[product-carbon-footprint]] from a proper [[life-cycle-assessment]], measured reductions, or certifications with substance such as [[b-corp]]. If you sell into the EU, the incoming EU rules are stricter still; see [[eu-green-claims-directive]]. When in doubt, say less and prove more; the alternative is [[greenwashing]] risk with a regulator that can now fine directly.
