**In short:** Scope 3 covers all the emissions your business causes but does not directly control: growing your ingredients, making your bottles and cans, hauling pallets, chilling your product in bars, and dealing with the packaging afterwards. For most drinks brands it is the vast majority of the footprint.

## What it covers

The [[ghg-protocol|GHG Protocol]] defines three scopes. [[scope-1-emissions|Scope 1]] is what you burn or leak yourself, [[scope-2-emissions|Scope 2]] is the energy you buy, and Scope 3 is everything else upstream and downstream of your business. It is organised into 15 categories, from purchased goods to end-of-life treatment of sold products, described plainly at [[scope-3-categories]].

Every tonne in your Scope 3 is someone else's Scope 1 or 2. That is deliberate: the point is not to double-count the world's emissions but to show each company the full consequences of its decisions.

## Why it dominates in drinks

A typical drinks brand does relatively little on site compared with what happens around it:

- **Packaging**, especially virgin glass bottles, is often the single largest slice.
- **Agriculture**: barley, grapes, apples, sugar, agave and botanicals carry emissions from fertiliser, land and machinery.
- **Logistics**: moving heavy, liquid-filled glass around the world adds up quickly.
- **Refrigeration downstream**: bars, retailers and consumers chilling your product.

For brands that outsource production entirely, essentially the whole footprint is Scope 3. See [[drinks-carbon-hotspots]] for the typical breakdown.

## How it is measured

Nobody measures Scope 3 perfectly. The standard approach is to start broad and refine:

1. **Spend-based estimates**: multiply what you spent by average [[emission-factors]] per pound. Crude but complete.
2. **Activity-based estimates**: use real quantities, such as tonnes of glass or kilometres trucked, with database factors.
3. **Supplier-specific data**: actual figures from your suppliers, such as a [[product-carbon-footprint]] for your bottle.

Moving up this ladder is the journey from [[primary-vs-secondary-data|secondary to primary data]], and it is normal for your reported footprint to change as data improves rather than because emissions changed. Be open about that.

The Scope 3 Standard itself is being revised, with a consultation draft due during 2026 and a final version expected around the end of 2027, so expect some rules to tighten.

## What this means for drinks businesses

- Do not wait for perfect data. A rough, complete Scope 3 estimate beats a precise account of the 5% you control directly.
- Focus effort where it counts: get real data for packaging, key ingredients and freight first.
- Ask suppliers for their numbers. Increasingly they will ask for yours too, because you are part of their Scope 3.
- Credible [[net-zero]] commitments and [[science-based-targets]] require you to address Scope 3, not just your own sites.
- Reduction levers include lighter bottles, higher recycled content, local sourcing, mode-shifting freight from air and road to sea and rail, and reformulating packaging altogether.
