**In short:** Several different gases warm the planet, some far more powerfully than carbon dioxide. CO2e (carbon dioxide equivalent) is the common currency that lets you add them all up into one number.

## The main greenhouse gases

- **Carbon dioxide (CO2)**: the most abundant, released mainly by burning coal, oil and gas. The reference gas everything else is measured against.
- **Methane (CH4)**: from livestock, rice, landfill and leaks in gas networks. Far more powerful than CO2 but shorter-lived in the atmosphere.
- **Nitrous oxide (N2O)**: largely from nitrogen fertiliser breaking down in soil, which is why it matters for anyone whose supply chain starts in a field.
- **Fluorinated gases (HFCs and others)**: synthetic gases used mainly as refrigerants. Some are hundreds or thousands of times more powerful than CO2, so even small leaks from chillers count.

## Global warming potential

Scientists compare gases using global warming potential (GWP): how much warming one kilogram of a gas causes relative to one kilogram of CO2, usually over 100 years. On that basis methane is roughly 28 times more powerful than CO2 and nitrous oxide roughly 265 times, with some common refrigerants in the thousands. The exact values shift slightly with each IPCC assessment report, which is one reason published [[emission-factors]] are updated regularly.

## What CO2e means

CO2e converts every gas into the amount of CO2 that would cause the same warming. Emit one tonne of methane and you record roughly 28 tonnes CO2e. This lets a [[carbon-footprint]] or [[product-carbon-footprint]] wrap boiler fuel, fertiliser and refrigerant leaks into a single comparable figure. When you see "kg CO2e per litre", that is [[carbon-intensity]]: total warming effect per unit of product.

One nuance worth knowing: CO2 released by fermentation is treated differently because the carbon came out of the air recently via the crop. That story lives at [[biogenic-carbon]].

## What this means for drinks businesses

- Your footprint is not just CO2. Fertiliser on barley, grapes or apples (N2O) and refrigerant leaks (HFCs) can be significant slices of [[scope-1-emissions|Scope 1]] and [[scope-3-emissions|Scope 3]].
- Cream liqueurs and anything dairy-based inherit substantial methane emissions from cattle.
- Ask your refrigeration engineer which gas your equipment uses. Replacing a high-GWP refrigerant, or simply fixing leaks, is one of the cheapest wins available.
- When reading supplier data, check it is in CO2e, not just CO2. The difference can be large for agricultural ingredients.
- You rarely need to calculate GWPs yourself. Published [[emission-factors]] already do the conversion; your job is good activity data.
