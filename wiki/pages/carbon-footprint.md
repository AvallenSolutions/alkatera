**In short:** An organisational carbon footprint is the total greenhouse gas emissions your business causes over a year, expressed as a single figure in tonnes of CO2e. It is built by multiplying things you can count, such as litres of fuel and tonnes of glass, by published conversion numbers.

## What goes into it

A footprint has three ingredients:

1. **A boundary**: which sites, companies and activities count as "you", and which reporting year you are measuring.
2. **Activity data**: the measurable facts of the year. kWh of electricity, litres of diesel, tonnes of ingredients, pallets shipped, flights taken.
3. **[[emission-factors|Emission factors]]**: published numbers that convert each activity into kilograms of CO2e, the common unit explained at [[greenhouse-gases-and-co2e]].

Multiply, sum, and you have a footprint. The method comes from the [[ghg-protocol|GHG Protocol]] Corporate Standard, which nearly every scheme and customer questionnaire builds on.

## The three scopes

Footprints are reported in three slices so companies never confuse what they control with what they influence:

- [[scope-1-emissions|Scope 1]]: direct emissions from your boilers, vehicles and refrigerant leaks.
- [[scope-2-emissions|Scope 2]]: emissions from generating the electricity and heat you buy.
- [[scope-3-emissions|Scope 3]]: everything else in the value chain, from barley fields to bottle banks.

For most drinks businesses Scope 3 is by far the largest, often 80 to 95% of the total. See [[drinks-carbon-hotspots]] for where it concentrates.

## Footprint of a company versus a product

An organisational footprint covers everything the business did in a year. A [[product-carbon-footprint]] measures one product across its life cycle, such as a single 70cl bottle. They answer different questions and use overlapping data, but they are not interchangeable. Dividing the total by production volume gives a carbon intensity figure, useful for tracking progress as you grow.

## Honest numbers

Every footprint contains estimates. Early footprints lean on spend data and industry averages; better ones use metered data and supplier figures, the shift described in [[primary-vs-secondary-data]]. Your number will change as data improves, which is progress, not failure. State your boundary, your data sources and what you left out.

## What this means for drinks businesses

- Start with one full year of energy bills, fuel receipts, purchase records and freight data. Completeness beats precision.
- Recalculate annually on the same boundary so year-on-year comparisons mean something.
- Expect to need this number: [[secr|SECR]] requires energy and emissions reporting for larger UK companies, larger customers increasingly demand it through their own Scope 3 work, and it underpins any net zero commitment and most sustainability reporting.
- Use the footprint to act, not just to disclose. The biggest lines in the spreadsheet are your reduction to-do list.
