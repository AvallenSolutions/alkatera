**In short:** The ESRS are the detailed rulebook that companies covered by the [[csrd]] must follow when writing their sustainability reports. They are being heavily simplified: a slimmed-down version is in the final stages of adoption in 2026 and is due to apply from financial year 2027.

## What they are

The European Sustainability Reporting Standards set out exactly what an in-scope company must disclose, from greenhouse gas emissions to workforce conditions. The first set was adopted by the European Commission in July 2023 as a delegated regulation, meaning it applies directly across the EU without national variations. The standards are drafted by EFRAG, an advisory body to the Commission.

## What they cover

The original set contains twelve standards: two cross-cutting ones covering general principles and general disclosures, five environmental (climate change, pollution, water, biodiversity, circular economy), four social and one on business conduct. The climate standard, E1, is the one most drinks businesses will encounter second-hand: it requires reporting companies to disclose [[scope-1-emissions|scope 1]], [[scope-2-emissions|scope 2]] and [[scope-3-emissions|scope 3]] emissions, broadly following the [[ghg-protocol]]. A company only reports on topics that matter to it, decided through a [[double-materiality]] assessment: what affects the business financially, and what the business does to people and the environment.

## The 2026 simplification

As part of the EU's Omnibus simplification, EFRAG delivered technical advice on a slimmed-down ESRS in December 2025. The European Commission then consulted on a draft replacement delegated act, informally called "ESRS 2.0", from 6 May to 3 June 2026. The draft cuts mandatory datapoints by more than 60 per cent compared with the 2023 standards. As of July 2026 the revised standards have not yet been formally adopted: the Commission has said it intends to adopt them quickly after the consultation, followed by a scrutiny period for the European Parliament and Council. The revised standards are intended to apply from financial year 2027, in step with the CSRD's narrowed scope.

## What this means for drinks businesses

Most drinks companies will never write an ESRS report themselves. What matters is that your large customers do, and ESRS E1 is why they ask suppliers for energy and emissions data, often at product level via a [[product-carbon-footprint]]. If a customer questionnaire cites ESRS datapoints, that is the source. For smaller businesses asked for data, the voluntary [[vsme]] standard is the sanctioned lighter-weight answer, and under the amended CSRD large companies are not supposed to demand more than it covers. Building good [[primary-vs-secondary-data|primary data]] on your own energy and ingredients now makes every future request cheaper to answer, whatever the final ESRS text says.
