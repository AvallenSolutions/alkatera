**In short:** Renewable energy certificates are tradeable proofs that one megawatt hour of electricity was generated from renewables. They are what lets a business claim its electricity is "green", but the claim is an accounting one, not a physical one.

## The certificate families

Each region has its own scheme, all built on the same idea: one certificate per megawatt hour of renewable generation.

- **REGOs** (Renewable Energy Guarantees of Origin) in Great Britain, issued by Ofgem.
- **GOs** (Guarantees of Origin) across the EU.
- **RECs and I-RECs** in North America and much of the rest of the world.

The electricity you physically receive is the same mix as your neighbours'; the certificate assigns you the right to count the renewable attributes of specific generation.

## How they interact with Scope 2

Under the [[ghg-protocol]], companies report [[scope-2-emissions|Scope 2]] two ways (see [[market-based-vs-location-based]]). The location-based figure uses the average grid [[emission-factors|emission factor]] and ignores certificates. The market-based figure reflects your contracts: electricity backed by valid certificates that meet the GHG Protocol's Scope 2 Quality Criteria (matching market, vintage, no double claiming, cancelled on your behalf) can be counted at the renewable generation's emission rate, often near zero. Both figures should be reported; the certificate only ever moves the market-based one.

## Limitations to be honest about

- **Additionality.** Unbundled certificates (bought separately from any power contract) are often extremely cheap, and there is credible evidence they rarely cause new renewable capacity to be built.
- **A claim, not a change.** Your site's physical electricity and the grid's actual emissions are unchanged; only the accounting allocation differs.
- **Strength hierarchy.** On-site generation and long-term power purchase agreements (PPAs) drive real new capacity; a green tariff backed by REGOs is legitimate but weaker; loose unbundled certificates are the weakest and attract [[greenwashing]] scrutiny if oversold.
- Standards are tightening: [[science-based-targets|SBTi]] and others are debating stricter matching of certificates to when and where you consume.

## What this means for drinks businesses

A renewables-backed tariff is a quick, legitimate first step that lowers your market-based Scope 2 and flows through to your [[carbon-footprint]] and customer questionnaires. Ask your supplier to confirm REGO backing in writing. But do not describe certificate-backed electricity as if you had built solar panels, keep reporting the location-based figure alongside, and treat efficiency and on-site generation as the substance behind any [[net-zero]] plan.
