**In short:** Primary data is measured from your actual operations and suppliers: your meter readings, your bottle's real weight, your maltster's own footprint. Secondary data is an industry average from a database. Every footprint starts mostly secondary and improves from there.

## The two kinds of data

- **Primary data** comes from the specific things in your value chain: metered energy, weighed packaging, fuel records from your haulier, or a [[product-carbon-footprint]] your glass supplier calculated for your exact bottle.
- **Secondary data** comes from published averages: [[emission-factors]] from databases such as the UK Government conversion factors, ecoinvent or Agribalyse, representing a typical bottle, a typical tonne of barley, a typical lorry.

Most real calculations mix both: a primary quantity (you know you bought 40 tonnes of glass) multiplied by a secondary factor (a database average for glass production).

## Why the difference matters

Averages hide variation. Malt from a gas-fired maltings and malt from a biomass-fired one can differ substantially; a lightweighted bottle with high [[recycled-content]] beats the database's "average glass bottle". While you use secondary data, your footprint tracks the industry, not you: your supplier could halve their emissions and your numbers would not move. Primary data is what connects your figures to reality, which is why the [[ghg-protocol|GHG Protocol]] Scope 3 guidance pushes companies towards supplier-specific data for their biggest categories, and why the revisions now in progress are expected to require clearer disclosure of how much of a footprint rests on which kind of data.

## The improvement journey

The practical sequence for [[scope-3-emissions|Scope 3]]:

1. **Screen** everything with spend-based estimates and database averages. Fast, crude, complete.
2. **Prioritise** the biggest lines, which for drinks usually means packaging, key ingredients and freight. See [[drinks-carbon-hotspots]].
3. **Replace** averages at those hotspots with real weights, distances, meter readings and supplier figures.
4. **Repeat** annually. Each cycle, a little more of the footprint becomes primary.

Expect your numbers to move as data improves, sometimes upwards. That is measurement working, not a scandal, but it does mean comparisons need care: note which changes came from better data rather than real-world reductions, and restate baselines when the difference is material.

## What this means for drinks businesses

- Cheap primary data first: weigh your actual bottles, cans and cases, and pull real energy figures from bills. No supplier negotiation required.
- Ask your top suppliers by spend for product-level footprints. Glass makers and maltsters increasingly have them ready.
- Record what is primary and what is secondary in your [[carbon-footprint]] workings. Customers and auditors will ask, and honest labelling of data quality is itself a credibility signal in [[sustainability-reporting]].
- Do not delay measuring until primary data exists. A complete estimate now, refined every year, beats a perfect number never.
