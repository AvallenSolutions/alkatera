**In short:** Biogenic carbon is carbon that was recently absorbed from the atmosphere by growing plants. When it returns to the air, for example when grain sugars ferment, it is counted differently from fossil carbon because it is part of a short natural cycle.

## Why it is treated differently

Burning fossil fuels adds carbon that was locked underground for millions of years. Releasing biogenic carbon returns carbon that the plant took from the atmosphere only months or years earlier. Over the crop cycle the flows roughly balance, so carbon accounting rules separate the two rather than lumping them together. Separate does not mean irrelevant: if land is cleared to grow the crop, or the biomass is never regrown, real emissions occur and are counted in full.

## How carbon accounting handles it

- Under the [[ghg-protocol]] corporate rules, biogenic CO2 (for example from burning biomass or from fermentation) is reported as a separate line outside [[scope-1-emissions|Scope 1]], [[scope-2-emissions|Scope 2]] and [[scope-3-emissions|Scope 3]], often labelled "outside of scopes". Methane and nitrous oxide from biological sources still count inside the scopes.
- In a [[product-carbon-footprint]] under ISO 14067 or the GHG Protocol Product Standard, biogenic uptake and release are tracked separately from fossil emissions, and land-use change emissions are always included.

## Fermentation CO2 in drinks

Fermentation converts sugars into alcohol and carbon dioxide, so every brewery, winery and distillery releases CO2 as part of making its product. That CO2 is biogenic: the barley, grapes, apples or molasses drew it from the atmosphere as they grew. It is therefore normally reported separately rather than added to the headline footprint.

Two honest caveats. First, "biogenic" is not a free pass; capturing fermentation CO2 for carbonation or sale displaces bought-in CO2, which is often a fossil by-product, so capture is still a genuine improvement. Second, growing the crop itself (fertiliser, diesel, land use) produces very real emissions that sit firmly inside the footprint; see [[drinks-carbon-hotspots]].

## What this means for drinks businesses

- Report biogenic CO2 as its own line in your [[life-cycle-assessment]] or footprint; do not silently delete it or quietly add it to Scope 1.
- If fermentation volumes are large, investigate CO2 capture and reuse.
- Avoid claims like "our CO2 is natural, so we are low carbon". That framing is [[greenwashing]].
