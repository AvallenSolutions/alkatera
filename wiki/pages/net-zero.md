**In short:** Net zero means reducing your emissions as close to zero as you can, roughly a 90% cut for most businesses, and then permanently removing the small amount that remains. Buying offsets instead of cutting emissions does not get you there.

## What net zero actually requires

"Net zero" comes from climate science: to stop global temperatures rising, the world must balance the greenhouse gases it adds to the atmosphere with the amount it takes out. For a business, the most widely accepted definition comes from the Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi), whose Corporate Net-Zero Standard requires two things:

1. **Deep cuts first.** Reduce emissions across [[scope-1-emissions|Scope 1]], [[scope-2-emissions|Scope 2]] and [[scope-3-emissions|Scope 3]] by around 90% by 2050 at the latest, with a near-term target along the way (typically cutting emissions by roughly half by 2030).
2. **Neutralise the residual.** The last few percent that genuinely cannot be eliminated must be counterbalanced by permanent carbon removals: projects that take carbon out of the atmosphere and store it durably. Cheaper "avoided emission" credits do not qualify for this step.

The order matters. Reduction is the substance of net zero. Removals are the finishing step for what is left, not an alternative to cutting.

## Net zero is not carbon neutral

A [[carbon-neutral]] claim usually means a company has bought [[carbon-offsetting|offsets]] equal to its current emissions. That can be done tomorrow, with no reduction at all. Net zero cannot: it demands that the emissions themselves fall by roughly 90% first. Regulators and standards bodies increasingly treat loose "net zero" claims made without a credible reduction plan as [[greenwashing]].

## What this means for drinks businesses

For most drinks producers, the bulk of the [[carbon-footprint]] sits in Scope 3: packaging, ingredients and logistics (see [[drinks-carbon-hotspots]]). A credible net zero commitment therefore means engaging suppliers, redesigning packaging and rethinking distribution, not just switching the brewery, distillery or winery to renewable electricity. Practical steps:

- Measure your full footprint, including Scope 3, before setting any target.
- Set a near-term reduction target first, and consider having it validated through [[science-based-targets]].
- Treat carbon removals as a future budget line for residual emissions, not a shortcut available today.
- Check any public net zero claim against the [[uk-green-claims-code]] before you publish it.
