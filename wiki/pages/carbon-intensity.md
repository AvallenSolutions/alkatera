**In short:** Carbon intensity expresses emissions relative to something: per litre produced, per bottle, per tonne, or per pound of revenue. It shows how efficiently you operate, but a falling intensity can still hide rising total emissions.

## Common intensity metrics in drinks

- **Per litre (or hectolitre) of product:** the workhorse metric for producers, comparable across years and sites.
- **Per container or per serving:** intuitive for consumers and useful alongside a [[product-carbon-footprint]]; always state the pack size and the [[functional-unit]].
- **Per £ of revenue or per employee:** favoured by finance teams and required contexts; the UK's [[secr]] regime obliges qualifying companies to publish at least one intensity ratio of their choice.

Whichever you use, the underlying measurement is the same [[carbon-footprint]] built on the [[ghg-protocol]]; intensity is just that total divided by an activity figure.

## Why intensity and absolute both matter

Intensity answers "are we getting more efficient?" Absolute emissions answer "are we emitting less?" The atmosphere only responds to the second. A brand that doubles production while cutting intensity by 20% has still substantially increased its total emissions. The reverse trap exists too: shrinking sales can flatter absolute numbers while efficiency worsens.

Good practice, and the expectation of [[science-based-targets]] and any credible [[net-zero]] plan, is to track both: intensity to steer operations and compare products fairly, absolute to judge real progress. Reporting only whichever number looks better in a given year is a recognisable [[greenwashing]] pattern.

## What this means for drinks businesses

- Pick one primary intensity metric (kg CO2e per litre is a sensible default), define it once and keep it stable across years.
- Publish intensity and absolute figures together, with the base year stated.
- Use intensity to find problems: a site or SKU with an outlying figure usually points to a fixable issue in packaging, energy or yield (see [[drinks-carbon-hotspots]]).
- When growth is the plan, set absolute reduction targets anyway; efficiency alone will not deliver them.
