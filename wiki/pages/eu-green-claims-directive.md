**In short:** The Green Claims Directive was the EU's proposed law requiring companies to prove environmental claims before making them. Its progress collapsed in 2025 and, as of July 2026, it is stalled. But do not relax: a separate, already adopted law, the Empowering Consumers Directive, starts applying on 27 September 2026 and bans many common green claims outright.

## What it is

Proposed by the European Commission in March 2023, the Green Claims Directive would have required companies making explicit environmental claims to consumers (for example "made with 30 per cent less carbon") to substantiate them with evidence and have that evidence verified by an independent third party before publication. It would also have tightened the rules on environmental labels and logos.

## Who it applies to

Jurisdiction: the European Union. As drafted, it would have covered most businesses making voluntary environmental claims in consumer marketing, with the treatment of micro-enterprises one of the main sticking points.

## Key dates

- March 2023: Commission proposal published.
- 2024 to early 2025: Parliament and Council agreed negotiating positions; trilogue talks began.
- 20 June 2025: the Commission announced its intention to withdraw the proposal; days later Italy pulled its support and negotiations halted.
- 21 October 2025: the Commission's 2026 work programme still listed the proposal as pending rather than formally withdrawn.

## Current status

As of July 2026, plainly: the Green Claims Directive is dormant. It has not been formally withdrawn, but no negotiations are taking place and there is no adoption timetable. Businesses should not build compliance plans around it.

The rules that ARE coming are in the Empowering Consumers for the Green Transition Directive (Directive (EU) 2024/825), adopted in February 2024. Member states had to transpose it by 27 March 2026, and its rules apply from 27 September 2026. It bans generic claims such as "environmentally friendly", "green" or "eco" unless recognised excellent environmental performance can be demonstrated, bans claims that a product has a neutral, reduced or positive climate impact when based on [[carbon-offsetting|offsetting]], and restricts sustainability labels to those based on approved certification schemes.

## What this means for drinks businesses

From September 2026, a "[[carbon-neutral]]" label justified by purchased offsets is banned in EU consumer marketing, and vague words like "sustainable" or "eco-friendly" on a bottle, can or website are legally risky across the EU. This catches [[greenwashing]] whether or not it is intentional. Claims that survive are specific, evidenced ones, backed by data such as a [[product-carbon-footprint]] built on a proper [[life-cycle-assessment]], or verified progress against [[science-based-targets]]. UK trading rules are similar in spirit; see the [[uk-green-claims-code]].
