**In short:** A circular economy keeps materials in use instead of making, using and binning them. For drinks businesses the practical battleground is packaging: how bottles, cans, kegs and cases are designed, returned and remade.

## The idea

Today's economy is mostly linear: extract materials, make products, throw them away. A circular economy follows three principles: design out waste and pollution, keep products and materials in use at their highest value, and regenerate natural systems. The point is not recycling more; it is needing less material in the first place and losing less of it at end of life.

## The hierarchy for drinks packaging

Not all "circular" actions are equal. As a rule of thumb, in descending order of impact:

1. **Reduce.** Lightweight the container, remove unnecessary layers and gift boxes. The lowest-carbon packaging is the packaging you did not use; glass weight is usually the single biggest lever (see [[drinks-carbon-hotspots]]).
2. **Reuse and refill.** Returnable glass, steel kegs, refill stations and bulk shipping with local filling. A returnable bottle spreads its production footprint over many trips, though washing and return logistics must be counted honestly; benefits depend on high return rates and short distances.
3. **Recycle.** Design for real-world recyclability (mono-materials, sensible labels and closures) and buy back [[recycled-content]] to close the loop.
4. **Recover.** Energy recovery from what remains is a last resort, not a circular outcome.

## Policy is pushing the same direction

Regulation increasingly rewards circular design: the [[eu-packaging-regulation-ppwr]] sets reuse and recyclability requirements, [[extended-producer-responsibility]] fees scale with how recyclable your packaging is, [[deposit-return-schemes]] lift collection rates for drinks containers, and the [[uk-plastic-packaging-tax]] penalises plastic without recycled content. Circularity is shifting from marketing language to cost line.

## What this means for drinks businesses

- Audit your packaging portfolio: weight, material, recycled content, real recyclability where it is actually sold.
- Model reuse honestly before switching: return rate, wash energy and transport distance decide whether a returnable system beats a light single-use one. A [[life-cycle-assessment]] comparing the options prevents expensive wrong turns.
- Design for the systems that exist: a container that suits your markets' deposit and recycling infrastructure will cost less under EPR fees.
- Beware circular-sounding claims without numbers; "100% recyclable" where no collection exists is [[greenwashing]].
