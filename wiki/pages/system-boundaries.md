**In short:** System boundaries define where a footprint calculation starts and stops along a product's life. Two footprints with different boundaries measure different things, so the boundary must always be stated alongside the number.

## The main boundaries

- **Cradle-to-gate** covers everything from raw material extraction and farming through production, up to the point the finished product leaves your gate. It excludes distribution, retail, use and disposal.
- **Cradle-to-grave** covers the full life: cradle-to-gate plus transport to market, refrigeration at retail or in the home, and end-of-life treatment of the packaging.
- **Gate-to-gate** covers only the stages within one facility or company, for example just the brewery's own operations. Useful internally, rarely meaningful externally.

You will also see hybrids such as cradle-to-shelf. The vocabulary comes from [[life-cycle-assessment]] practice under ISO 14044 and the [[ghg-protocol]] Product Standard.

## Which boundary for which purpose

- **Sharing data with business customers:** cradle-to-gate is the norm, because your customer adds the downstream stages (their logistics, their retail) inside their own [[scope-3-emissions|Scope 3]] accounting. Double counting the same stages helps nobody.
- **Consumer-facing claims and labels:** cradle-to-grave is usually the honest choice, since a shopper reasonably assumes the number covers the product's whole life, including the bottle's disposal. A cradle-to-gate number presented without qualification can mislead; see [[greenwashing]].
- **Process improvement inside the business:** gate-to-gate comparisons are fine, as long as they never leak into marketing dressed up as full footprints.

## What this means for drinks businesses

For most packaged drinks the difference between boundaries is large: packaging end-of-life, chilled distribution and retail refrigeration sit outside cradle-to-gate but can be significant (see [[drinks-carbon-hotspots]]). Practical rules:

- Publish the boundary, the [[functional-unit]] and the base year with every figure.
- Never compare your cradle-to-gate number with a competitor's cradle-to-grave one, in either direction.
- When customers request a [[product-carbon-footprint]], confirm the boundary they expect before you calculate, not after.
