**In short:** Offsetting means paying for climate action outside your own business, one carbon credit per tonne of CO2e, to compensate for emissions you have not cut. Done well it funds useful projects. Done badly it buys a misleading claim.

## How offsets and credits work

A carbon credit represents one tonne of carbon dioxide equivalent ([[greenhouse-gases-and-co2e|CO2e]]) said to be avoided or removed by a project. Credits are issued by registries such as Verra and Gold Standard, then bought and "retired" by companies who want to claim the benefit. Retirement matters: an unretired credit can be resold, so only retirement supports any claim.

## Avoidance vs removal

- **Avoidance credits** pay for emissions that would otherwise have happened: protecting forest at risk of clearance, funding renewable energy or clean cookstoves. They depend heavily on hypothetical baselines, which is where most quality scandals have arisen.
- **Removal credits** pay for taking carbon out of the atmosphere: woodland creation, soil carbon, biochar, or engineered approaches such as direct air capture. Genuine removals are scarcer and more expensive, and they are the only type that counts towards neutralising residual emissions under [[net-zero]] standards.

## What makes a credit credible

The Integrity Council for the Voluntary Carbon Market (ICVCM) sets a widely used quality benchmark, the Core Carbon Principles. The essentials:

- **Additionality:** the project would not have happened without the credit revenue.
- **Permanence:** the carbon stays stored, with safeguards against reversal (fire, felling, failure).
- **Conservative measurement:** no inflated baselines.
- **No double counting:** one tonne, one claim, one owner.
- **Independent verification** by an accredited third party.

## Credible use, and the pitfalls

Reduce first. The [[science-based-targets|SBTi]] does not allow offsets to count towards reduction targets. Its recommended role for credits is "beyond value chain mitigation": funding climate action in addition to cutting your own emissions, not instead of it, and describing it as a contribution rather than as neutralisation.

The pitfalls are well documented: very cheap credits with over-stated baselines, double-sold tonnes, and marketing claims that collapse under scrutiny. Offset-based [[carbon-neutral]] product claims are being banned in the EU and are high-risk [[greenwashing]] territory under the [[uk-green-claims-code]] and the proposed [[eu-green-claims-directive]].

## What this means for drinks businesses

- Spend on reductions in your own [[drinks-carbon-hotspots|hotspots]] before spending on credits; the money usually goes further.
- If you do buy credits, favour high-quality removals or ICVCM-approved credits, keep the paperwork, and say "we fund climate projects" rather than "this bottle is neutral".
- Never let an offset purchase delay the harder work on packaging, ingredients and energy.
