**In short:** For most packaged drinks, the biggest emissions are not where people expect. Packaging, especially single-use glass, and agriculture usually outweigh everything that happens inside the brewery, distillery or winery.

## Where the emissions concentrate

Every product differs, and the honest answer starts with measuring your own [[product-carbon-footprint]]. That said, industry studies and published [[life-cycle-assessment|LCAs]] point to a consistent pattern for packaged drinks:

- **Packaging** is frequently the largest single contributor, often somewhere between a quarter and half of a bottled product's footprint, and sometimes more for premium spirits in heavy glass. Glass is energy-intensive to melt and heavy to move; cans, kegs and bag-in-box typically carry less burden per litre, though the comparison depends on [[recycled-content]] and return rates.
- **Agriculture and ingredients** come next: fertiliser production and the nitrous oxide it releases in the field, farm diesel and land use behind barley, grapes, apples, sugar and botanicals. For wine and cider, the vineyard or orchard share is often larger than the winery's.
- **Process energy**: heat for mashing, boiling and especially distillation, which is inherently energy-hungry, plus site electricity. Winemaking uses comparatively little process heat. Fermentation CO2 itself is [[biogenic-carbon]] and is counted separately.
- **Refrigeration and dispense**: cold chains, cellar cooling and retail fridges, including leakage of refrigerant gases, which are potent greenhouse gases. Significant for beer and soft drinks served cold.
- **Logistics**: usually smaller than people assume for sea and road freight of efficient loads, but heavy packaging multiplies it, and air freight is ruinous.

Treat all proportions as indicative. Category, pack format, distance to market and data quality shift the picture substantially, which is why two credible studies of similar products can disagree.

## Practical reduction levers

1. **Attack packaging first.** Lightweight the glass, switch formats where the brand allows, raise recycled content, and consider reuse systems (see [[circular-economy]]).
2. **Buy better ingredient data, then better ingredients.** Replace industry-average [[emission-factors]] with [[primary-vs-secondary-data|primary data]] from your maltster, growers or co-operative, and work with them on fertiliser efficiency and regenerative practices.
3. **Decarbonise heat and power.** Renewable electricity helps [[scope-2-emissions|Scope 2]] quickly; heat recovery, mechanical vapour recompression in distilling and biogas address the harder [[scope-1-emissions|Scope 1]] fuel use.
4. **Fix refrigeration.** Efficient equipment, low-GWP refrigerants and leak checks.
5. **Ship dense and slow.** Fuller pallets, lighter packs, rail and sea over road and air.

## Where to start

Measure one representative SKU properly, find its top three contributors, and act there. Most drinks businesses discover that the majority of their footprint sits in [[scope-3-emissions|Scope 3]], bought goods rather than their own fuel, which reframes the job as supplier engagement rather than just site efficiency.
