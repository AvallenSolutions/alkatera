**In short:** A science-based target is an emissions reduction target checked by an independent body, the Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi), to confirm it matches what climate science says is needed. It is the most widely recognised way to prove a climate commitment is real.

## What the SBTi is

The SBTi is an independent organisation, founded by CDP, the UN Global Compact, the World Resources Institute and WWF, that publishes target-setting standards and validates company targets against them. Over 10,000 companies have targets or commitments. Validation gives a claim credibility that a self-declared pledge lacks.

## Near-term and net-zero targets

- **Near-term targets** cover the next 5 to 10 years and must align with limiting warming to 1.5°C. In practice that means cutting [[scope-1-emissions|Scope 1]] and [[scope-2-emissions|Scope 2]] emissions by roughly 42% by 2030. Larger companies must also address [[scope-3-emissions|Scope 3]] where it dominates their footprint.
- **Net-zero targets** commit to the long game: emissions cut by around 90% by 2050 at the latest, with only the residual neutralised by permanent removals. See [[net-zero]] for why this is not the same as offsetting. [[carbon-offsetting|Offsets]] cannot be counted towards either target type.

## The route for smaller businesses

SMEs get a streamlined route: a simplified online validation process with predefined targets rather than a bespoke submission. The core near-term requirement is a cut of at least 42% in Scope 1 and 2 emissions by 2030 against a recent base year, plus a commitment to measure and reduce Scope 3 without needing a quantified Scope 3 target. It is significantly cheaper and faster than the corporate route.

## Corporate Net-Zero Standard V2.0

The SBTi published Version 2.0 of its Corporate Net-Zero Standard on 11 June 2026, the biggest revision since 2021. It introduces size and geography categories: large companies face the fullest requirements, while small and micro businesses (Category B) get deliberately proportionate ones, with near-term Scope 3 targets optional. Companies can submit targets under V2.0 from early 2027; both V2.0 and the previous version (V1.3.1) are accepted until 31 January 2028, after which V2.0 becomes mandatory for new submissions.

## What this means for drinks businesses

- Measure first: you need a [[carbon-footprint]] built on the [[ghg-protocol]] before you can set a target.
- Most independent breweries, distilleries and brands qualify for the SME route, which removes most of the cost and paperwork.
- Expect the question from customers: large retailers and distributors with their own validated targets increasingly ask suppliers to set targets too, because you sit in their Scope 3.
- Since packaging and ingredients usually dominate, delivering a target means working on [[drinks-carbon-hotspots]], not just your own site's energy.
