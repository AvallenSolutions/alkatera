**In short:** Life cycle assessment (LCA) is the structured way to measure a product's environmental impact across its whole life, from growing the raw materials to disposing of the packaging. It is the method behind every credible [[product-carbon-footprint]].

## Why "life cycle" matters

Judging a product by one stage misleads. A lightweight plastic pouch looks better than glass at the factory gate but worse at end of life; an organic ingredient may need more land. LCA exists to stop this whack-a-mole by counting every stage, so an improvement in one place is not just an export of the problem to another.

## The four phases

The international standards ISO 14040 and ISO 14044 define four phases:

1. **Goal and scope**: decide the question. What product, measured how, compared with what, and where the study starts and stops. This is where you fix the [[functional-unit]] (the "per what", such as per 70cl bottle) and the [[system-boundaries|system boundary]] (cradle-to-gate or cradle-to-grave).
2. **Inventory analysis**: list everything flowing in and out across the life cycle: kilograms of barley, grams of glass, kWh of energy, kilometres of freight, litres of water.
3. **Impact assessment**: convert those flows into impacts using [[emission-factors]] and equivalent conversion data. Climate change is the headline, but LCA also covers water use, land use and other categories; see [[water-footprint]].
4. **Interpretation**: work out what the results mean, test how sensitive they are to assumptions, and state the limitations honestly.

In practice the phases loop: interpretation often sends you back to improve the inventory, typically by replacing database averages with supplier figures, the move described in [[primary-vs-secondary-data]].

## LCA and carbon footprints

When an LCA looks only at greenhouse gases, the result is a carbon footprint. ISO 14067 is the standard written specifically for the carbon footprint of products, built on the ISO 14040 family. So "we did an LCA" and "we have a PCF" usually describe the same exercise at different widths.

## What this means for drinks businesses

- You do not need to run an LCA by hand; software and platforms do the mechanics. You do need to supply honest input data, because results are only as good as the recipe, packaging weights and energy figures you provide.
- Ask any provider two questions: what is the functional unit, and where is the boundary? If they cannot answer plainly, be wary.
- Use LCA results for decisions, such as bottle weight, [[recycled-content]] and sourcing, before using them for marketing.
- Comparative claims ("lower impact than X") carry the highest evidential bar under ISO rules and advertising regulation. See [[uk-green-claims-code]].
- Expect uncertainty. Two competent LCAs of the same drink can differ on assumptions alone. Consistent method over time matters more than any single figure.
