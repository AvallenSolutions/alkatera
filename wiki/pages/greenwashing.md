**In short:** Greenwashing is making a product or business look more environmentally friendly than it really is. It is often accidental, a marketing team overstating a genuine improvement, but regulators treat the effect on the consumer as what matters, not the intent.

## Common patterns

- **Vague terms.** "Eco-friendly", "green", "sustainable" and "planet-positive" with no definition or evidence.
- **Hidden trade-offs.** Highlighting one improvement (a lighter bottle) while staying silent on a bigger impact it worsens or ignores.
- **Unsubstantiated claims.** Statements no document in the business can actually back up, or that rely on unpublished, cherry-picked data.
- **Irrelevant claims.** Advertising the absence of something that was never there, or something every competitor also does.
- **Misleading imagery.** Leaves, oceans and earth tones implying benefits the product does not have.
- **Offset-backed absolutes.** "Carbon neutral" or "climate positive" badges resting on cheap credits; see [[carbon-neutral]] and [[carbon-offsetting]].

## Why it matters more than it used to

Environmental claims are now regulated as consumer protection. In the UK, the [[uk-green-claims-code]] sets out six principles (truthful, clear, complete, comparable, substantiated, full life cycle) and the CMA can act under consumer law. In the EU, the Empowering Consumers Directive bans generic claims and offset-based neutrality claims from September 2026, and the [[eu-green-claims-directive]] would require pre-approval of claims in some cases. Beyond fines, the commercial damage of a public ruling usually outlasts the campaign.

## How to avoid it

- Be specific: "bottle weight cut 18% since 2024" rather than "greener packaging".
- Hold the evidence before you publish, ideally a [[product-carbon-footprint]] or [[life-cycle-assessment]], and keep it current.
- Describe the whole picture: a [[recycled-content|recycled]] label does not offset a heavier container.
- Make uncertainty visible. "We estimate" is honest; false precision is not.
- Check claims against both the UK code and EU rules if you export.

## What this means for drinks businesses

Drinks labels are small, crowded and consumer-facing, which makes them a greenwashing hotspot: recyclability logos, "sustainably sourced" flashes and neutrality badges all attract regulator attention. The safe path is fewer, sharper claims backed by data, and ambitions framed honestly: a validated target under [[science-based-targets]] or progress towards [[net-zero]] says more than any leaf icon. Good [[sustainability-reporting]] discipline internally is the best defence externally.
