**In short:** The PPWR is the EU's new packaging law. It starts applying on 12 August 2026 and will, over time, require all packaging to be recyclable, set minimum recycled content in plastic bottles, and require a share of drinks to be sold in reusable packaging. Anyone selling packaged drinks into the EU is affected, including exporters based outside it.

## What it is

The Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation (Regulation (EU) 2025/40) replaces the 1994 Packaging Directive. Because it is a regulation rather than a directive, it applies directly and identically in every member state, ending the patchwork of national packaging design rules. It covers packaging design, recyclability, recycled content, labelling, reuse, and extended producer responsibility.

## Who it applies to

Jurisdiction: the European Union. It applies to everyone placing packaging or packaged products on the EU market: manufacturers, importers, brand owners and distributors. A UK or US drinks brand exporting to the EU must comply for those products, and each member state still runs its own [[extended-producer-responsibility]] scheme underneath it.

## Key dates

- 11 February 2025: entered into force.
- 12 August 2026: most provisions apply, including a declaration of conformity for packaging placed on the market and restrictions such as the PFAS ban in food-contact packaging.
- 1 January 2029: member states must run [[deposit-return-schemes|deposit return schemes]] for single-use plastic bottles and metal drinks containers, unless they already collect over 80 per cent separately.
- 1 January 2030: recyclability design criteria bite (all packaging must meet design-for-recycling grades); minimum [[recycled-content|recycled content]] in plastic packaging applies, including 30 per cent for single-use plastic drinks bottles; reuse targets start, with 10 per cent of beverages to be offered in reusable packaging, rising towards an indicative 40 per cent by 2040.

## Current status

In force, applying from 12 August 2026. Much of the detail (recyclability grading, labelling formats) arrives through secondary legislation still being finalised, so specifics for 2028 to 2030 will keep firming up.

## What this means for drinks businesses

Drinks packaging is squarely targeted: PET bottles, cans, glass and secondary packaging. The 2030 reuse targets apply to beer, cider, soft drinks and ready-to-drink products, but wine, aromatised wine products, spirit drinks and milk are exempt from them (a point to check per product, as review clauses exist). Recycled-content minimums will tighten PET supply and prices, an effect that compounds with the [[uk-plastic-packaging-tax]] for UK businesses. Since packaging is one of the biggest [[drinks-carbon-hotspots|carbon hotspots in drinks]], designing lighter, recyclable, higher-recycled-content packaging now serves both compliance and your [[product-carbon-footprint]], and moves you towards a genuinely [[circular-economy|circular]] model rather than a paper exercise.
