**In short:** Scope 2 emissions come from generating the electricity, heat, steam or cooling your business buys. The smoke rises at the power station, not at your brewery or distillery, but your energy use causes it, so you count it.

## What counts as Scope 2

Anything you purchase as delivered energy:

- Grid electricity for your site, offices, taproom or cellar cooling.
- Purchased heat or steam, for example from a district heating network or a shared industrial site.
- Purchased cooling, which is rarer but treated the same way.

Fuel you burn yourself is [[scope-1-emissions|Scope 1]]. Energy used by your suppliers, or by pubs and shops chilling your product, belongs in [[scope-3-emissions|Scope 3]]. The [[ghg-protocol|GHG Protocol]] designed the scopes this way so a given emission is only ever one company's Scope 1.

## Two ways to count it

The GHG Protocol requires companies to report Scope 2 in two parallel ways:

- **Location-based**: your kWh multiplied by the average [[emission-factors|emission factor]] for your local grid. This reflects the physical reality of the wires you are plugged into.
- **Market-based**: a figure that reflects your energy contracts, such as a renewable tariff backed by [[renewable-energy-certificates|renewable energy certificates]] or a power purchase agreement.

The two numbers can differ a lot, and each answers a different question. The full explanation lives at [[market-based-vs-location-based]]. Note that the rules here are being revised: the GHG Protocol's Scope 2 Guidance went through public consultation ending in January 2026, with updated standards expected around the end of 2027.

## How it is measured

Take the kWh from your electricity bills or half-hourly meter data and multiply by the relevant grid factor. In the UK that factor is published every June in the Government conversion factors and falls most years as the grid adds more renewables and retires fossil generation.

## What this means for drinks businesses

- Collect 12 months of energy bills for every site, including offices, warehouses and hospitality venues. Estimates are acceptable for small gaps, but say so.
- Refrigeration, cellar cooling and compressed air are typically the biggest electricity users in drinks production and hospitality. Metering them separately shows where the money and carbon go.
- A renewable tariff reduces your market-based figure, but check what actually backs it before making public claims. Weak certificates plus bold claims is a fast route to [[greenwashing]] accusations.
- Efficiency first: reducing kWh cuts both reported figures and your bills, whatever the contract says.
- UK companies in [[secr|SECR]] must report Scope 2, and energy use in kWh, alongside [[scope-1-emissions|Scope 1]].
- Keep perspective: for most drinks brands, Scope 2 is far smaller than [[scope-3-emissions|Scope 3]] from packaging and ingredients. See [[drinks-carbon-hotspots]].
