**In short:** There are two ways to count the emissions from electricity you buy. Location-based uses the average for your local grid. Market-based reflects what your contract says you bought, such as a renewable tariff. The [[ghg-protocol|GHG Protocol]] says report both.

## Location-based: the grid you plug into

The location-based method multiplies your kWh by the average [[emission-factors|emission factor]] of the grid you are physically connected to. It reflects reality at the wall socket: electrons are mixed on the grid, so everyone in a region shares the same average. It changes only when the grid itself gets cleaner or you use less electricity.

## Market-based: the contracts you signed

The market-based method reflects your purchasing choices, evidenced by contractual instruments:

- **Green tariffs** from your supplier.
- **[[renewable-energy-certificates|Renewable energy certificates]]**: in Great Britain these are REGOs; in Europe, Guarantees of Origin. Each certificate represents one MWh of renewable generation.
- **Power purchase agreements (PPAs)**: long-term contracts with a specific wind or solar project.

Electricity matched by such instruments counts at the generator's emission rate, often zero. Anything unmatched should use a "residual mix" factor, the grid average after all the claimed renewable generation is removed, which is higher than the plain average.

## Why both numbers matter

The two figures answer different questions. Location-based: what do my operations physically cause? Market-based: what have I chosen to pay for? A brewery on a standard tariff and its neighbour on a certificate-backed tariff have identical location-based figures but very different market-based ones. Critics note that cheap unbundled certificates can lower a reported number without adding any new renewable generation to the grid, which is why the [[ghg-protocol|GHG Protocol]] requires dual reporting rather than letting companies show only their better number. These rules are currently under revision: the Scope 2 Guidance consultation closed in January 2026 and updated standards are expected around the end of 2027, with tighter quality criteria widely anticipated.

## What this means for drinks businesses

- Report both figures for [[scope-2-emissions|Scope 2]], clearly labelled. Showing only the flattering one invites [[greenwashing]] challenges.
- Ask your supplier what backs your "green" tariff. A tariff backed only by leftover certificates is weaker evidence than a PPA or on-site solar.
- Do not let a zero market-based figure end the conversation. Reducing kWh through efficiency cuts both numbers, your bills and actual emissions.
- If you make public claims like "powered by 100% renewable electricity", keep the certificates and contract evidence on file; see the [[uk-green-claims-code]].
- Remember this debate only affects Scope 2, usually a small slice of a drinks [[carbon-footprint]]. Do not spend more energy on electricity accounting than on packaging and ingredients.
