**In short:** A water footprint measures how much fresh water a product or business consumes and pollutes. For drinks, water is both the main ingredient and a production input, but most of the footprint usually sits in the fields, not the factory.

## Measuring water in drinks production

The simplest site metric is the **water intensity ratio**: litres of water used per litre of product made. It covers everything drawn on site, including cleaning, rinsing, cooling and the liquid itself. Efficient large breweries get close to 3 litres per litre; smaller and older sites are often well above that, and distilleries typically draw much more because of cooling water, most of which is returned rather than consumed. Treat any benchmark with caution: technology, product mix and site age move the numbers a lot.

A full water footprint under ISO 14046 works like a [[life-cycle-assessment]]: it follows the product across its life, per [[functional-unit]], and weights water use by local scarcity rather than just adding litres.

## Blue, green and grey water in brief

The Water Footprint Network's vocabulary splits water into three colours. **Blue** water is surface and groundwater withdrawn, for irrigation or brewing. **Green** water is rainwater held in soil and used by crops. **Grey** water is the volume needed to dilute pollution to acceptable levels. For most drinks, green water on the farm dwarfs everything else, which is why ingredient sourcing dominates the total, much as it does for carbon (see [[drinks-carbon-hotspots]]).

## Risk matters more than volume

A thousand litres drawn from a wet catchment is not the same as a thousand litres from a stressed one. Water risk is local: the same barley or grapes grown in a water-scarce region carry a very different real-world impact. Tools such as WWF's Water Risk Filter and WRI Aqueduct map catchment stress. This is why serious water strategies talk about "water stewardship" in specific catchments rather than a single global number, and why [[sustainability-reporting]] frameworks increasingly ask where your water comes from, not just how much.

## What this means for drinks businesses

- Meter and track your site ratio monthly; it is cheap, and leaks, cleaning-in-place routines and pack rinsing usually offer quick wins.
- Map your ingredient origins against catchment stress before claiming your water use is low; use [[primary-vs-secondary-data|primary data]] from growers where you can.
- Report volume and context together. "4.1 litres per litre, sites in low-stress catchments" is a credible statement; a bare total is not, and cherry-picking the factory number while ignoring irrigated crops shades into [[greenwashing]].
- Water and carbon interact: heating, treating and pumping water costs energy, so savings often show up in your [[carbon-footprint]] too.
