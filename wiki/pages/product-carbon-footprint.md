**In short:** A product carbon footprint (PCF) is the total greenhouse gas emissions caused by one unit of one product across its life, for example one 70cl bottle of gin from the barley field to the bottle bank, expressed in kg CO2e.

## How it differs from a company footprint

A [[carbon-footprint|company footprint]] answers "how much did our whole business emit this year?". A PCF answers "how much does one bottle cause?". The PCF follows the product wherever it goes, including stages your company never touches, such as retail chilling and packaging disposal.

## How one is built

A PCF is a focused application of [[life-cycle-assessment]]. The essential decisions:

- **The [[functional-unit]]**: exactly what "one unit" means. One 70cl bottle at 40% ABV is not comparable to one litre of ready-to-drink at 5%. Comparisons only work when the unit matches.
- **The [[system-boundaries|system boundary]]**: where the study starts and stops. "Cradle-to-gate" covers everything up to leaving your site; "cradle-to-grave" continues through distribution, use and disposal. A grave-less footprint is not wrong, but it must say what it excludes.
- **The data**: quantities of ingredients, packaging, energy and transport, multiplied by published emission factors. Supplier-specific figures beat database averages; see [[primary-vs-secondary-data]].

The recognised methods are the [[ghg-protocol|GHG Protocol]] Product Standard and ISO 14067, the international standard specifically for carbon footprints of products. They differ in detail but agree on the fundamentals.

## What the results usually show

For most drinks, packaging and agriculture dominate. A virgin glass bottle frequently outweighs everything else, with ingredients, transport and processing following. The pattern is mapped at [[drinks-carbon-hotspots]]. Fermentation CO2 is treated separately as [[biogenic-carbon]].

## What this means for drinks businesses

- Start with your best seller. One well-understood PCF teaches you more than ten rough ones, and its hotspots usually apply across the range.
- Use the PCF to make decisions: lighter bottles, higher recycled content, different formats, closer suppliers. It is a design tool, not just a certificate.
- Expect customers to ask. Retailers and distributors increasingly request PCFs to feed their own [[scope-3-emissions|Scope 3]] accounting.
- Be careful with the number in public. A footprint on a label invites comparison, and comparative green claims are regulated; see the [[uk-green-claims-code]] before printing anything.
- Record your assumptions. When your data improves and the number moves, you will need to explain why. That transparency is what separates measurement from marketing.
