**In short:** Scope 1 emissions are the greenhouse gases your business releases directly from things it owns or controls: the gas burned in your boiler or still, the diesel in your delivery van, and the refrigerant gas that leaks from your chillers.

## Where the term comes from

The [[ghg-protocol|GHG Protocol]], the rulebook behind most carbon accounting, splits a company's emissions into three "scopes" so that no two companies count the same emissions in the same scope. Scope 1 is the direct slice. [[scope-2-emissions|Scope 2]] covers purchased energy and [[scope-3-emissions|Scope 3]] covers everything else in your value chain.

## What counts as Scope 1

- **Stationary combustion**: fuel burned in equipment on your sites, such as gas boilers, steam raising for mashing or distillation, and direct-fired stills.
- **Mobile combustion**: petrol and diesel burned in vehicles your company owns or leases, including forklifts, delivery vans and company cars.
- **Fugitive emissions**: gases that escape rather than being burned. The big one for drinks businesses is refrigerant leaking from chillers, cellar cooling and air conditioning. Many refrigerants are extremely powerful greenhouse gases, so a small leak can matter a lot. See [[greenhouse-gases-and-co2e]].
- **Process emissions**: gases released by an industrial process itself rather than by burning fuel.

One drinks-specific wrinkle: the carbon dioxide released by fermentation is biogenic, meaning it comes from carbon the crop absorbed while it grew. It is tracked separately rather than added to your Scope 1 total. See [[biogenic-carbon]] for how that works.

## What does not count

Electricity you buy is [[scope-2-emissions|Scope 2]]. Emissions from your suppliers, from hauliers you pay, from employee commuting and from making your packaging all sit in [[scope-3-emissions|Scope 3]]. The test is ownership or control: if a courier owns the van, those emissions are the courier's Scope 1 and your Scope 3.

## How it is measured

Multiply activity data (litres of diesel, kWh of gas, kilograms of refrigerant topped up) by published [[emission-factors]], such as the UK Government conversion factors. Scope 1 is usually the easiest scope to measure well because the evidence sits in your own fuel bills and service records.

## What this means for drinks businesses

- Gather fuel invoices, vehicle fuel card statements and refrigeration service records; together these cover most of Scope 1.
- Ask your refrigeration engineer to record the gas type and top-up amount at every service. Top-ups are how leaks are quantified.
- Breweries and distilleries with gas-fired boilers usually find heat is their largest on-site source, though for most brands the whole site is still small next to [[scope-3-emissions|Scope 3]]. See [[drinks-carbon-hotspots]].
- Scope 1 is the scope you control most directly, so it is often where reduction projects start: boiler efficiency, heat recovery, fleet electrification and switching to low-impact refrigerants.
- If you report under [[secr|SECR]] in the UK, Scope 1 is a mandatory part of the disclosure.
