**In short:** B Corp is a certification run by the non-profit B Lab that verifies a company meets standards of social and environmental performance, transparency and accountability. Since 2025 the standards have changed fundamentally: instead of scoring 80 points, companies must now meet requirements on every core topic.

## How certification worked, and what changed

For years, certification meant scoring at least 80 points on the B Impact Assessment (BIA), a questionnaire covering governance, workers, community, environment and customers. A company could excel in some areas and be weak in others, as long as the total cleared 80.

B Lab published new standards on 8 April 2025. The points model is gone. Companies must now meet **Foundation Requirements** (including eligibility checks and a legal commitment to stakeholder governance, typically written into the company's articles) plus defined sub-requirements across **seven Impact Topics**, including climate action, fair work and human rights. Performance in one topic can no longer compensate for another.

Certification also becomes a journey rather than a one-off pass: companies meet a set of Year 0 requirements to certify, then further requirements at Year 3 and Year 5, with goals set and progress tracked annually.

## Timing for existing and new B Corps

Existing B Corps are moving across as they recertify. Those due to recertify from 2026 onwards transition to the new standards and were given a 12-month extension to prepare. New applicants now certify under the new standards. Requirements scale with company size, so a ten-person brand faces a lighter version than a multinational.

## What this means for drinks businesses

B Corp is well established in drinks, and buyers recognise it. Practical points:

- The climate action topic expects you to measure your [[carbon-footprint]], and larger companies to set credible targets; work aligned with [[science-based-targets]] and [[net-zero]] slots straight in.
- Expect evidence requests across packaging, sourcing and people, not just carbon. Existing [[sustainability-reporting]] makes this far cheaper.
- The legal governance change is real: UK companies amend their articles of association to commit to all stakeholders.
- Budget serious time. Even under the old model, preparation typically took months; the new continuous-improvement model rewards starting your data collection early, especially [[scope-3-emissions|supply chain data]].
- Certification is a floor, not a marketing free-for-all: overselling it drifts into [[greenwashing]].
