**In short:** A carbon neutral claim means a company has calculated its emissions and bought offsets to match them. Because the emissions themselves may not have fallen at all, regulators increasingly restrict how the term can be used.

## How a carbon neutral claim works

The typical recipe: measure a [[carbon-footprint]] for the company or product, reduce it if you choose to, then buy [[carbon-offsetting|carbon credits]] equal to whatever remains and declare the result "neutral". Historically many claims were certified against the PAS 2060 specification, which is being replaced by the stricter ISO 14068-1 standard.

The weakness is built into the recipe: offsetting is compulsory, reduction is optional. A business can grow its emissions year after year and remain "carbon neutral" as long as it keeps buying credits.

## Why regulators are clamping down

- Investigations have repeatedly found that many cheap avoidance credits deliver far less climate benefit than claimed.
- To a shopper, "carbon neutral" implies the product has no climate impact. It does.
- In the EU, Directive 2024/825 (the Empowering Consumers Directive) bans product claims of neutral, reduced or positive environmental impact that rely on offsetting. It applies from 27 September 2026, so it is weeks away at the time of writing. The [[eu-green-claims-directive]] would add detailed substantiation rules on top.
- In the UK, the [[uk-green-claims-code]] requires claims to be truthful, substantiated and complete, and the Advertising Standards Authority has upheld complaints against offset-based neutrality advertising.

## Carbon neutral vs net zero

[[net-zero]] requires deep emission cuts of around 90% before any neutralising of residuals. Carbon neutral requires no cuts at all. The two terms are often used interchangeably in marketing; they should not be.

## What this means for drinks businesses

- If you sell into the EU, plan to remove offset-based "carbon neutral" wording from labels and marketing before 27 September 2026.
- Prefer specific, evidenced claims: "we reduced the footprint of this bottle by 24% since 2023" beats a neutrality badge.
- If you buy credits, describe the purchase honestly as a contribution to climate projects, not as cancelling out your product.
- Focus effort on genuine reductions in your [[drinks-carbon-hotspots|hotspots]], and treat vague neutrality badges as a [[greenwashing]] risk, not an asset. A validated target through [[science-based-targets]] carries far more weight with buyers.
