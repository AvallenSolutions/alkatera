## Core concepts

- [[b-corp]]: B Corp certification verifies a company's social and environmental performance. New standards from 2025 replace the 80-point score with mandatory topics.
- [[carbon-footprint]]: The total greenhouse gas emissions a business causes in a year, built from activity data and emission factors across Scopes 1, 2 and 3.
- [[carbon-offsetting]]: Carbon offsetting funds projects that avoid or remove emissions elsewhere. Credit quality varies hugely and offsets cannot replace cutting your own emissions.
- [[circular-economy]]: A circular economy designs out waste by keeping materials in use. For drinks that means lighter, reusable and genuinely recyclable packaging.
- [[emission-factors]]: The published conversion numbers that turn things you can count, like litres of diesel or tonnes of glass, into greenhouse gas emissions.
- [[greenhouse-gases-and-co2e]]: The main gases that warm the planet, how their strength is compared, and what the unit CO2e actually means.
- [[greenwashing]]: Greenwashing means making environmental claims that mislead, deliberately or not. Regulators now treat it as a consumer protection issue with real penalties.
- [[life-cycle-assessment]]: The structured method for measuring a product's environmental impacts across its whole life, standardised in ISO 14040 and ISO 14044.
- [[market-based-vs-location-based]]: The two required ways to count purchased electricity emissions: the average of your local grid, or the specifics of your energy contract.
- [[primary-vs-secondary-data]]: The difference between measured, supplier-specific data and database averages, and why footprints get more accurate over time.
- [[product-carbon-footprint]]: The total greenhouse gas emissions of a single product across its life cycle, such as one bottle of gin from field to disposal.
- [[science-based-targets]]: The SBTi validates company climate targets against climate science, with near-term and net-zero tracks and a simpler, cheaper route for smaller businesses.
- [[scope-1-emissions]]: Direct greenhouse gas emissions from sources your business owns or controls, such as boilers, stills, company vehicles and leaking refrigerants.
- [[scope-2-emissions]]: Emissions from generating the electricity, heat or steam your business buys. They happen off site, but your energy use causes them.
- [[scope-3-emissions]]: All the emissions in your value chain beyond your own sites and energy. For most drinks brands this is by far the biggest share of the footprint.
- [[scope-3-categories]]: The GHG Protocol splits Scope 3 into 15 categories. Here is each one in plain English, and which ones matter most for drinks businesses.
- [[water-footprint]]: A water footprint measures the water a drink consumes across its life. Where the water comes from matters as much as how much is used.

## Standards and methods

- [[esrs]]: The detailed rulebook companies use to report under the CSRD. A simplified "ESRS 2.0" is in final adoption during 2026, applying from financial year 2027.
- [[ghg-protocol]]: The family of standards behind most corporate carbon accounting, covering company footprints, purchased energy, value chains and products.
- [[vsme]]: The EU's voluntary sustainability reporting standard for SMEs, backed by a 2025 Commission Recommendation. One format to answer big customers' data requests.

## Legislation and compliance

- [[csrd]]: The EU law requiring large companies to publish audited sustainability reports. After the 2026 Omnibus changes it applies above 1,000 employees.
- [[deposit-return-schemes]]: UK deposit return schemes for drinks containers launch on 1 October 2027. Producers must register, label and charge deposits; many EU states already run DRS.
- [[eu-green-claims-directive]]: The EU's proposed law on proving environmental claims stalled in 2025. The adopted Empowering Consumers Directive applies from 27 September 2026 instead.
- [[eu-packaging-regulation-ppwr]]: The EU Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation applies from 12 August 2026, with recycled-content and reuse targets for drinks packaging from 2030.
- [[extended-producer-responsibility]]: UK packaging EPR makes producers pay the full cost of household packaging waste, with fees modulated by recyclability from 2026. EU states run own schemes.
- [[secr]]: UK rule requiring quoted and large companies to report energy use and emissions in their annual report. A 2026 review recommends keeping and simplifying it.
- [[uk-green-claims-code]]: The CMA's rules for environmental claims in UK marketing. Since April 2025 the CMA can fine misleading claims up to 10% of global turnover directly.
- [[uk-plastic-packaging-tax]]: UK tax of £228.82 per tonne (from April 2026) on plastic packaging with less than 30% recycled content, for manufacturers and importers over 10 tonnes a year.

## Glossary

- [[biogenic-carbon]]: Biogenic carbon comes from plants that absorbed CO2 while growing. Carbon accounting tracks it separately from fossil carbon, including fermentation CO2.
- [[carbon-intensity]]: Carbon intensity is emissions per unit, per litre, per bottle or per pound of revenue. Intensity shows efficiency; absolute emissions show climate impact.
- [[carbon-neutral]]: Carbon neutral claims balance emissions with offsets rather than cutting them, and UK and EU regulators are tightening the rules on using the term.
- [[double-materiality]]: Double materiality asks two questions: how your business affects people and planet, and how sustainability issues affect your business financially.
- [[functional-unit]]: The functional unit is the reference unit an LCA measures against, such as one litre of packaged drink. It is what makes comparisons fair.
- [[net-zero]]: Net zero means cutting emissions by around 90% and permanently removing the small remainder. It is much more demanding than carbon neutral.
- [[recycled-content]]: Recycled content is the share of a pack made from recovered material. Glass, aluminium and PET behave very differently, and tax and EU rules now set minimums.
- [[renewable-energy-certificates]]: REGOs, Guarantees of Origin and RECs certify renewable electricity. They lower a market-based Scope 2 figure but have well-known limitations.
- [[system-boundaries]]: System boundaries define which life cycle stages a footprint includes: cradle-to-gate, cradle-to-grave or gate-to-gate. Always state which one you used.
